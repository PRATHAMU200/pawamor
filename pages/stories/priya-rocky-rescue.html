Rocky's Second Chance

From Mumbai's Streets to a Loving Home

Priya Sharma Mumbai, India Indie Dog October 2024

The Day Everything Changed

It was a sweltering July afternoon in Mumbai when I first saw Rocky. I was returning from work, navigating through the crowded streets of Dadar, when I noticed a small brown dog lying motionless near a dumpster. At first, I thought he was just another street dog resting in the shade, but something about the way he lay there—so still, so defeated—made me stop.

As I approached, I could see he was in bad shape. His ribs were visible through his patchy, mange-ridden coat. One of his hind legs was clearly injured, possibly from a vehicle. But what broke my heart most were his eyes—they were full of pain, yet still held a glimmer of hope, as if he was waiting for someone, anyone, to help.

"I've never been a 'dog person.' I grew up in a household where pets were considered an unnecessary expense. But something about Rocky's eyes that day changed everything I thought I knew about myself."

I called a local animal rescue helpline, but they said they were overwhelmed with cases and couldn't send someone immediately. I couldn't just leave him there. Using my dupatta, I carefully created a makeshift sling and, with the help of an auto-rickshaw driver, transported Rocky to the nearest vet clinic.

The Diagnosis

Dr. Mehta, the veterinarian, examined Rocky thoroughly. The news wasn't good. Rocky had severe mange, multiple wounds from street fights, was severely malnourished, and his hind leg had an old fracture that had healed incorrectly. He also tested positive for tick fever.

"He'll need extensive treatment," Dr. Mehta said gently. "Mange treatment alone will take 2-3 months. The leg might always cause him a limp. And he'll need constant care during recovery. Are you prepared for this?"

I looked at Rocky, who was now lying on the examination table, looking up at me with those same hopeful eyes. In that moment, I realized I couldn't walk away. I had come too far. This dog, this brave survivor, deserved a chance.

"Yes," I heard myself say. "I'll take care of him."

The First Month: A Test of Patience

Those first few weeks were incredibly challenging. I set up a small space in my apartment's balcony for Rocky, as he couldn't be around my roommates (and I wasn't sure how they'd react). I woke up early every morning to medicate him, feed him, and clean his wounds before work.

Rocky was terrified of everything. Sudden movements made him flinch. Loud noises sent him cowering into the corner. He wouldn't eat from my hand, only if I left the food and walked away. The street had taught him that humans were a threat.

But slowly, painfully slowly, trust began to build. It started with food. On day five, he didn't wait for me to walk away before eating. On day twelve, he took a treat from my hand for the first time. On day eighteen, he wagged his tail when I arrived home from work.

"That first tail wag was better than any promotion or achievement in my life. It meant he was starting to see me not as a threat, but as a friend."

The Turning Point

The real breakthrough came during a thunderstorm about a month after I found Rocky. Mumbai's monsoon rains are intense, and that night, thunder crashed so loudly that even I felt frightened. I rushed to the balcony to check on Rocky.

I found him trembling violently in the corner, his eyes wide with terror. Without thinking, I sat down next to him, speaking softly. To my surprise, instead of moving away, he pressed against me. For the first time, Rocky sought comfort from a human.

We sat together like that for hours, through the entire storm. By the morning, something had fundamentally shifted between us. Rocky had decided I was safe. He had decided I was his.

Transformation

Over the next few months, Rocky's physical transformation was remarkable. The mange treatment worked wonders—his coat grew back thick and golden-brown. With proper nutrition, he gained healthy weight. His wounds healed. While his hind leg would always have a slight limp, he learned to run and play despite it.

But his emotional transformation was even more beautiful. The scared, defeated dog from the street became playful, affectionate, and surprisingly mischievous. He developed a favorite spot on my bed (I eventually gave up trying to keep him off it). He learned to play fetch with a squeaky toy. He appointed himself the official greeter for anyone who visited our apartment.

My initially skeptical roommates fell in love with him. "We need to admit," Neha said one evening as Rocky performed his signature "welcome home" dance, "this dog has made our apartment feel like a home."

Rocky the Therapy Dog

Here's something I never expected: Rocky started helping me with my anxiety. I work in a high-stress corporate job, and I've struggled with anxiety for years. But having Rocky meant I had to come home, had to take breaks for his walks, had to stay present for his needs.

On days when work stress felt overwhelming, Rocky seemed to sense it. He'd rest his head on my lap, look up at me with those expressive eyes, and somehow, my breathing would slow down. My racing thoughts would quiet. His presence was grounding in a way I can't fully explain.

Dr. Mehta later told me that street dogs, especially those who've been rescued, often develop incredible emotional intelligence. "They've survived by reading human body language and emotions," he explained. "Rocky can probably sense your stress better than most people."

Advocacy and Purpose

Rocky's rescue changed more than just our lives. It gave me purpose beyond my corporate job. I started volunteering with local animal rescue groups on weekends. I became an advocate for adopting Indian street dogs—Indies, as they're lovingly called.

Indies are incredibly hardy, intelligent, and loyal. They've evolved over thousands of years to survive India's diverse climates and conditions. Yet they're often overlooked in favor of expensive foreign breeds. Many people don't realize that the perfect dog might be waiting on their own street corner.

Through social media, I started sharing Rocky's journey. The response was overwhelming. People sent messages about how Rocky inspired them to help street dogs in their neighborhoods. Some adopted their own Indies. Others started feeding and caring for street dogs in their areas.

"Rocky didn't just get a second chance at life. He gave me one too. He showed me that love isn't something you go searching for in expensive breeds or perfect pedigrees. Sometimes, it's waiting for you in the most unexpected places—scared, wounded, but still hopeful."

Today

It's been a year and a half since that July afternoon. Rocky is unrecognizable from the dog I found by the dumpster. He's healthy, happy, and so loved. He has a wardrobe of colorful bandanas (he somehow looks regal in all of them). He has a collection of toys that rivals most children's. He has three "aunties" (my roommates) who spoil him rotten.

But more importantly, Rocky has a home. He has security. He has someone who will always choose him, always come back for him, always love him.

People often say to me, "You saved Rocky's life." But here's the truth: Rocky saved mine. He taught me about resilience—how something can be broken and still put itself back together. He taught me about trust—how it must be earned gently, patiently. He taught me about love—how it doesn't need to be perfect, just real.

Every morning, Rocky greets me with the same enthusiasm, as if each day is a miracle. And maybe it is. Every day above ground, safe and loved, is a miracle for a dog who wasn't expected to survive the streets.

The street dogs of India—the Indies—are survivors. They're warriors. They deserve our respect, our compassion, and yes, our homes. If you're thinking about adding a dog to your family, I urge you to look around your neighborhood first. Your Rocky might be out there, waiting for their second chance.

Advice for Potential Street Dog Rescuers

If Rocky's story inspired you, here's what I learned:

1. Start with a Vet Visit: Always get the dog checked medically before bringing them home. Street dogs often have treatable but contagious conditions.

2. Patience is Everything: Street dogs have trauma. They won't trust immediately. Give them time and space.

3. Prepare Your Home: Create a safe space where the dog can retreat. They need to feel secure.

4. Small Steps: Don't overwhelm them with attention. Let them come to you.

5. Connect with Rescue Groups: Organizations like YODA (Youth Organization in Defence of Animals) and local rescue groups can provide support and guidance.

6. Be Ready for Challenges: Medical treatment, behavioral issues, and adjustment take time and money. But I promise you, it's worth every rupee and every moment.

About Priya Sharma

Marketing professional based in Mumbai. First-time dog parent to Rocky and advocate for Indian street dog adoption. Volunteer with local animal rescue organizations. Believes that the best things in life are rescued.
