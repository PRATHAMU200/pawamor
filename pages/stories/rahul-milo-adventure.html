Milo's Midnight Mission

The Night a Beagle Pulled Off the Great Kitchen Heist

Rahul Mehta Pune, India Beagle October 2024

The Setup

Let me tell you about the night Milo, our two-year-old Beagle, became a legend in our family. This is the story we tell at every gathering now, the one that makes my mother-in-law laugh so hard she cries, the one my kids beg to hear again and again. It's become family folklore: "The Great Kitchen Heist of 2024."

To understand why this story is so epic, you need to understand a few things about Milo. First, he's a Beagle, which means he's 60% nose, 30% appetite, and 10% mischief. Second, he's smart—too smart for his own good. And third, he's absolutely, unequivocally, completely obsessed with food.

Not just any food, mind you. Milo has gourmet tastes. He'll turn his nose up at regular dog treats, but show him a samosa? A piece of paneer tikka? Mom's homemade laddoos? He transforms into a furry little ninja, capable of incredible feats of agility and cunning.

The Day Before

It was Diwali week, and my wife Priya had spent the entire day making sweets for the festival. Our kitchen counter was covered with plates of various delicacies: besan laddoos, coconut burfi, kaju katli, chocolate sandesh, and her signature dish—mawa pedas that literally melt in your mouth.

Milo had been circling the kitchen like a shark all day, his nose twitching, his eyes following every movement. We were careful, of course. We always kept food out of his reach. The counter was high, the chairs were pushed in, and we'd even put him in the bedroom when we left for the evening puja at our neighbor's house.

"He'll be fine," Priya said. "He can't reach the counter, and we'll only be gone an hour."

Famous last words.

"Never underestimate a Beagle with a mission. Their determination is matched only by their appetite."

The Crime Scene

We came home to what can only be described as a disaster movie set. Picture this: our living room looked like a tornado had hit it. The kitchen stool was in the middle of the room. A trail of coconut shavings led from the kitchen to the couch. And sitting in the middle of this chaos, looking like the cat who ate the canary (except he's a dog who ate approximately 2 kilograms of various sweets), was Milo.

His face was covered in chocolate. His paws were sticky with syrup. And his belly—oh his belly—was so round and distended that he looked like a furry little balloon.

"Milo!" Priya shrieked. "What did you DO?"

Milo burped. Loudly. The smell of mixed Indian sweets wafted across the room. Then he wagged his tail, as if to say, "Best. Night. Ever."

Piecing Together the Heist

It took us a while to figure out exactly what happened, but here's what we reconstructed based on the evidence:

Step 1: The Door: Somehow, Milo had learned to turn the bedroom door handle. We found scratch marks on the door and the handle was down. This dog, who we thought was safely contained, had let himself out.

Step 2: The Stool: Beagles can't reach kitchen counters. Milo knew this. So he'd dragged the kitchen stool from the corner (we found drag marks on the floor) and positioned it next to the counter.

Step 3: The Climb: He'd jumped on the stool, then onto the counter. We found paw prints on the stool cushion and on the counter itself. This was premeditated. This was planned.

Step 4: The Feast: Once on the counter, Milo had gone to town. He'd eaten: five laddoos, half a plate of coconut burfi, most of the chocolate sandesh, several mawa pedas, and—his biggest achievement—an entire plate of kaju katli that had cost ₹800.

Step 5: The Getaway: He'd knocked several plates onto the floor in his enthusiasm, pushed the stool away (probably when jumping down), and retired to the couch to enjoy his food coma.

"The evidence of his crime was everywhere. The guilt in his eyes? Nowhere to be found. He looked absolutely proud of himself."

The Aftermath

My first instinct was panic. "Priya, chocolate is poisonous for dogs! We need to get him to a vet NOW!"

At 10 PM on a Diwali weekend, finding an open vet clinic was a challenge. We called every emergency vet number we had. Finally, Dr. Sharma answered. I explained the situation.

"How much chocolate are we talking about?" he asked.

"Uh, maybe 200 grams of chocolate sandesh?"

"And he weighs?"

"About 12 kilos."

There was a pause. "Bring him in, but honestly, Indian sweets don't have enough pure chocolate to be severely toxic. The bigger problem is going to be the sugar and the dairy. He's going to have a very upset stomach."

Dr. Sharma was right. Milo wasn't in danger, but he was about to have the worst 24 hours of his life. The vet gave him something to make him vomit (not pleasant for anyone involved), and then we took our very sorry-looking Beagle home.

The Karma

If you think Milo got away with it, you'd be wrong. The universe had its revenge. Over the next day, Milo experienced what can only be described as doggy food poisoning times ten. Without going into too much graphic detail, let's just say our garden became a crime scene of a different sort, we went through an entire roll of paper towels, and Milo's expression went from "Best night ever" to "Why did I do this to myself?"

We slept in shifts to watch him, took him outside every hour, and spent more time cleaning up after him than we'd spent cleaning the initial kitchen disaster.

"I think he's learned his lesson," my 10-year-old daughter Aanya said sympathetically as Milo lay on his bed, looking miserable.

Spoiler alert: He absolutely had not learned his lesson. But more on that later.

The Family Bonding

Here's the thing though—while the heist itself was a disaster, the aftermath became one of our favorite family memories. We stayed up together that whole night, taking turns watching Milo, sharing stories about his previous mischief, laughing about the absurdity of the situation.

Priya stopped being mad (mostly) and started seeing the funny side. "I mean, you have to appreciate the planning that went into this. He's like a furry little Ocean's Eleven."

My son Aarav made a PowerPoint presentation called "Milo's Heist: A Timeline" with reconstructed events. My daughter drew comics about it. We took photos of the sad, bloated Milo and created a "Hall of Shame" photo album.

In a weird way, Milo's midnight adventure brought us all together. In our busy lives, we rarely spent entire nights as a family anymore. But there we were, at 3 AM, sitting in the living room, eating leftover laddoos (the ones Milo hadn't gotten to), and laughing until we cried.

"Sometimes the best family moments come from the most chaotic situations. Milo didn't just steal food that night—he gave us a story we'll treasure forever."

The Security Upgrades

Of course, we couldn't let this happen again. We implemented what we now call the "Milo-Proofing Protocol":

1. Door Locks: We installed hook-and-eye latches on all doors, high enough that Milo can't reach them even standing on his hind legs.

2. Stool Removal: No more stools in the kitchen when we leave. They're locked in a closet.

3. Counter Cleared: Nothing tempting gets left on counters. Ever.

4. The Milo Cam: Aarav set up an old phone as a pet camera so we can check on Milo when we're out. This has provided hours of entertainment watching him try and fail to access various things.

5. Pre-Departure Treats: We make sure Milo has a Kong stuffed with peanut butter before we leave. The theory is that a busy Beagle is a non-thieving Beagle.

The Legend Continues

It's been three months since the Great Kitchen Heist, and Milo is back to his normal, mischievous self. He's attempted several smaller heists since then (the Great Shoe Caper, the Sock Incident, the Case of the Missing Roti), but nothing quite as spectacular as that Diwali night.

The story has taken on a life of its own. My mother tells it at her kitty party. Priya's colleagues ask for updates on Milo's latest adventures. Our kids' friends come over asking, "Is that THE Milo? The one who ate all the sweets?"

And Milo? He basks in his infamy. When guests come over and we tell the story, he wags his tail, as if he knows we're talking about him. I swear sometimes he looks at the kitchen counter with a gleam in his eye, planning his next big score.

The Lesson

People ask me if we regret getting a Beagle. Are they too much work? Too mischievous? Too food-obsessed?

My answer is always the same: Absolutely not. Yes, Milo is a handful. Yes, he's caused us stress, cost us money in vet bills and destroyed sweets, and keeps us constantly on our toes. But he's also brought so much joy and laughter into our home.

Before Milo, our family dinners were quiet, everyone on their phones. Now we share "What did Milo do today?" stories. Before Milo, our weekends were separate activities. Now we go on family walks because Milo needs exercise. Before Milo, we took ourselves too seriously. Now we can laugh at ourselves and at the chaos a 12-kilogram dog can create.

"Milo didn't just steal sweets that night. He stole our hearts. And honestly? We wouldn't have it any other way."

A Warning to Future Beagle Owners

If you're considering getting a Beagle, here's what you need to know:

They're Escape Artists: If there's a way out, they'll find it. Locks are essential.

They're Food Motivated: And I mean REALLY food motivated. Never underestimate what they'll do for a snack.

They're Clever: Too clever. They can open doors, drawers, and possibly filing taxes (okay, maybe not that last one).

They're Stubborn: Training takes patience. "Come" means "maybe in a minute" to a Beagle.

They're Vocal: The Beagle howl is legendary. Your neighbors will know you have a Beagle.

But here's what else you need to know:

They're Loving: Milo greets us like we've been gone for years every time we come home.

They're Playful: His enthusiasm for life is contagious.

They're Family Dogs: They want to be part of everything. Loneliness isn't in their vocabulary.

They're Funny: Life with a Beagle is never boring.

So yes, get a Beagle. Just maybe hide your sweets first.
