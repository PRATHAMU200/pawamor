Training Philosophy: Positive Reinforcement

Dog training isn't about dominance or punishment—it's about communication, trust, and making good behavior rewarding. Positive reinforcement training builds a strong, loving bond while teaching your dog to make good choices.

What is Positive Reinforcement?

Positive reinforcement means rewarding behaviors you want to see more of. When your dog does something right, they get something they love (treats, praise, play), making them more likely to repeat that behavior.

Why Positive Training Works

  • ✅ Builds trust and strengthens your bond
  • ✅ Dogs learn faster when motivated by rewards
  • ✅ Creates a happy, confident dog
  • ✅ No fear or anxiety associated with learning
  • ✅ Teaches dogs to think and make choices

Avoid Punishment-Based Methods

Research shows punishment-based training (yelling, hitting, shock collars) can cause:

  • Fear and anxiety
  • Aggression and defensive behavior
  • Damaged human-dog relationship
  • Suppressed symptoms without addressing root cause
  • Long-term behavioral problems

Training Mindset

Dogs aren't being "stubborn" or "spiteful"—they simply don't understand what you want yet. Training is teaching, not forcing. Patience, consistency, and positivity are your best tools.

Training Basics & Setup

The 3 Pillars of Successful Training

1. Consistency

  • Use the same commands/cues every time
  • All family members must use same rules and techniques
  • Practice daily, even if just 5-10 minutes
  • Don't allow behaviors sometimes but not others

2. Timing

  • Reward within 2 seconds of desired behavior
  • Use marker words ("Yes!" or clicker) to mark exact moment
  • Delayed rewards confuse dogs about what earned the treat

3. Patience

  • Training takes time—puppies especially have short attention spans
  • Break learning into small, achievable steps
  • End sessions on a positive note
  • If frustrated, take a break

Essential Training Tools

  • High-Value Treats: Small, soft, smelly (boiled chicken, cheese, commercial training treats)
  • Clicker (Optional): Makes timing easier—marks exact behavior
  • Proper Collar/Harness: Comfortable, well-fitting
  • 6-Foot Leash: For training (not retractable)
  • Treat Pouch: Keeps treats handy during training
  • Toys: For play-motivated dogs

Training Session Structure

  1. Short Sessions: 5-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily
  2. Distraction-Free: Start in quiet room, gradually add distractions
  3. Before Meals: Hungry dogs are more motivated by food
  4. High Energy First: Brief exercise before training helps focus
  5. End Positively: Finish with command dog knows well

Don't Overtrain

Long, repetitive sessions lead to frustration and boredom. Multiple short, fun sessions throughout the day are much more effective than one exhausting marathon.

Essential Commands Step-by-Step

1. Name Recognition

Why First: Your dog must learn their name means "look at me" before other commands work.

Training Steps:

  1. Say dog's name once in happy voice
  2. When they look at you, mark it ("Yes!") and treat immediately
  3. Repeat 10-15 times per session
  4. Gradually increase distance and add distractions
  5. Never use name when scolding (keep it positive)

2. Sit

Foundation Command: Easiest to teach, prevents jumping, used before everything.

Lure Method:

  1. Hold treat close to dog's nose
  2. Move treat up and back over their head slowly
  3. As nose goes up, bottom goes down naturally
  4. The moment bottom touches ground, say "Yes!" and treat
  5. After 5-10 successes, add verbal cue "Sit" just before luring
  6. Gradually reduce lure motion, make it subtle
  7. Eventually phase out treat lure completely (still reward after!)

Pro Tips:

  • Don't push bottom down—let them figure it out
  • Keep treat close to nose so they don't jump up
  • Practice 20+ times daily in different locations

3. Down

Calm Position: Useful for settling, vet visits, restaurants.

Training Steps:

  1. Start with dog in sit position
  2. Hold treat at nose, slowly lower to ground between paws
  3. Dog will likely fold into down following treat
  4. If they stand up, restart—don't give treat for standing
  5. Mark and reward the instant elbows hit ground
  6. Add verbal "Down" cue once motion is reliable

Alternative Method (if dog won't lie down):

  • Sit on floor with one leg raised (knee bent)
  • Lure dog under your leg with treat
  • They'll have to lie down to fit under
  • Reward immediately

4. Stay

Duration & Impulse Control: Teaches patience and self-control.

Training Steps:

  1. Put dog in sit or down
  2. Say "Stay" with palm-out hand signal
  3. Wait 2-3 seconds, then mark and reward (toss treat to them)
  4. Gradually increase duration: 5 sec, 10 sec, 20 sec, etc.
  5. Once duration solid, add distance (one step back at a time)
  6. Then add distractions
  7. Use release word ("Okay!" or "Free!") to end stay

Common Mistakes:

  • ❌ Increasing difficulty too fast (work on duration OR distance, not both)
  • ❌ Forgetting release word—dog should stay until released
  • ❌ Repeating "stay" over and over (say once, wait for duration)

5. Come/Recall

Life-Saving Command: Most important for safety—must be reliable.

Training Steps:

  1. Never call dog to you for something unpleasant
  2. Start indoors with zero distractions
  3. Say name, then "Come!" in exciting, happy voice
  4. Run backwards to encourage chase (dogs love it)
  5. Massive reward when they reach you (jackpot—5-10 treats!)
  6. Practice with long leash outdoors before off-leash
  7. Gradually increase distance and distractions

Recall Rules

  • NEVER punish dog for coming to you (even if they did something bad first)
  • NEVER chase dog who won't come—you'll make it a game
  • ALWAYS make coming to you better than anything else
  • Use different word ("Here!") if "come" is poisoned from overuse

6. Leave It / Drop It

Safety Commands: Prevents eating dangerous items.

"Leave It" Training:

  1. Hold treat in closed fist
  2. Let dog sniff, lick, paw at hand
  3. Wait for them to give up and look away
  4. Mark that moment and treat from OTHER hand
  5. Repeat until they automatically look away from fist
  6. Add "Leave it" cue
  7. Practice with treat on floor (cover with foot if they go for it)

"Drop It" Training:

  1. Let dog take low-value toy
  2. Offer high-value treat at nose
  3. Most dogs drop toy to get treat
  4. Mark and reward, then give toy back
  5. Add "Drop it" cue before showing treat
  6. Gradually use less exciting treats

House Training (Potty Training)

The Golden Rules

  • Frequent Breaks: Puppies: Every 1-2 hours, after eating/playing/waking
  • Consistent Spot: Always take to same area
  • Immediate Rewards: Treat and praise the SECOND they finish (not after coming inside)
  • Supervision: When inside, watch constantly or use crate/pen
  • No Punishment: Rubbing nose in it or scolding teaches them to hide, not to go outside

Step-by-Step Process

1. Establish Routine

  • Take out immediately after: waking up, eating, drinking, playing
  • Use same door and path each time
  • Go to same spot in yard
  • Use cue word while they go ("Go potty")

2. Reward Success

  • Party time when they go outside!
  • Treat + lots of praise immediately
  • Then playtime or walk (bonus reward)

3. Prevent Accidents

  • Confine to one room when you can't supervise
  • Use crate training (dogs avoid soiling sleeping area)
  • Tether to you with leash indoors
  • Watch for signals: sniffing, circling, whining, going to door

4. Handle Accidents Properly

  • If caught in act: Calmly interrupt, take outside immediately
  • If discovered later: Clean up without reaction (they won't connect it)
  • Use enzymatic cleaner to remove all scent
  • Increase supervision and bathroom breaks

Crate Training Tips

  • Crate should be comfortable den, not punishment
  • Size: Just enough to stand, turn, lie down (not too big)
  • Feed meals in crate to build positive association
  • Never leave puppy crated longer than they can hold it (age in months + 1 = hours)
  • Always potty break immediately before and after crating

Patience Required

Puppies aren't fully reliable until 6-12 months old. Accidents happen—it's part of learning. Stay consistent, and they'll get there!

Leash Training & Walking

Goal: Loose Leash Walking

Dog walks calmly beside you with slack in leash—no pulling either direction.

Why Dogs Pull

  • Pulling gets them where they want to go (it works!)
  • Excited by outdoor stimulation
  • Walking faster than your pace
  • Never learned to walk calmly

Training Method: "Be A Tree"

Steps:

  1. Start walking with dog on 6-foot leash
  2. The INSTANT leash goes tight, stop moving (be a tree)
  3. Don't move forward until leash is slack
  4. When dog looks back or leash loosens, mark and treat
  5. Continue walking
  6. Repeat every single time leash tightens

What This Teaches: Pulling = no progress. Loose leash = we move forward.

Alternative Method: Direction Changes

  • When dog pulls ahead, immediately turn and walk opposite direction
  • Dog learns to pay attention to you, not just rush forward
  • Reward when they catch up and walk beside you

Practice "Check-Ins"

  • Reward dog for looking at you during walks
  • Say name → when they look, mark and treat
  • Creates habit of paying attention to you
  • Use high-value treats on walks (more distractions)

Equipment Options

  • Standard Flat Collar: Fine for non-pullers
  • Front-Clip Harness: Redirects pulling to side (easier on you)
  • Head Halter: Gives control like horse halter (requires conditioning)
  • Avoid: Choke chains, prong collars (cause pain/injury)

Training Walk vs. Exercise Walk

Separate training walks (focus on leash skills) from exercise walks (let them sniff and explore more). Training walks should be short, focused sessions.

Socialization

Critical Period: 3-14 Weeks

This is the most important window for socialization. Puppies are naturally curious and less fearful. Positive experiences now shape adult temperament.

What to Socialize To

  • People: Different ages, genders, ethnicities, clothing (hats, uniforms, wheelchairs)
  • Dogs: Various sizes, breeds, play styles (ensure positive, safe interactions)
  • Environments: Busy streets, parks, vet clinic, pet stores, car rides
  • Sounds: Traffic, thunder, vacuum, fireworks, doorbells
  • Surfaces: Grass, concrete, gravel, stairs, metal grates
  • Handling: Touching paws, ears, mouth (for grooming/vet care)

How to Socialize Properly

Golden Rules:

  • Positive experiences only—never force, watch for stress
  • Let puppy set pace—allow them to approach at their comfort level
  • Pair with treats—new thing = yummy treats
  • Keep it short—multiple brief exposures better than overwhelming
  • End on positive note—before puppy gets tired or scared

Signs of Good Socialization

  • Relaxed body language
  • Curiosity and willingness to explore
  • Takes treats readily
  • Recovers quickly from startling

Signs of Fear/Stress

  • Tail tucked, ears back
  • Refusing treats
  • Trying to hide or escape
  • Excessive panting, drooling
  • Freezing or shutting down

If you see stress signs: Create more distance, go slower, make it easier. End session and try again another day.

Vaccination Caution

Puppies need socialization but aren't fully vaccinated until 16 weeks. Solution: Carry puppy in busy areas, invite vaccinated dogs to your home, puppy classes with health requirements. Balance disease risk with behavioral needs (undersocialized dogs are more likely to be surrendered).

Common Problem Behaviors

1. Jumping on People

Why they do it: Seeking attention, excited greeting behavior

Solution:

  • Ignore jumping completely (turn away, fold arms, no eye contact)
  • When four paws on floor, mark and reward with attention
  • Ask dog to sit before greeting
  • Teach "off" command (reward when they back away)
  • Consistency is key—everyone must follow same rules

2. Mouthing/Biting (Puppies)

Why they do it: Natural puppy behavior, teething, play

Solution:

  • Yelp loudly and stop playing when teeth touch skin
  • Redirect to appropriate chew toys
  • Reward gentle play
  • Time-outs if it continues (leave room briefly)
  • Never encourage mouthing during play

3. Barking

Identify the cause:

  • Alert barking: Teach "quiet" command, reward silence
  • Attention barking: Ignore completely until quiet
  • Boredom barking: Increase exercise and mental stimulation
  • Anxiety barking: Address underlying anxiety

4. Destructive Chewing

Prevention:

  • Puppy-proof environment
  • Provide appropriate chew toys
  • Supervise or crate when you can't watch
  • Exercise dog adequately (tired dog = good dog)
  • Rotate toys to keep them interesting

5. Separation Anxiety

Signs: Destructive behavior, excessive barking, house soiling only when alone

Approach:

  • Start with very short separations (30 seconds)
  • Gradually increase duration
  • Leave special toys/treats for alone time only
  • Don't make big deal of departures/arrivals
  • Consider calming aids (music, pheromones)
  • Severe cases need professional behaviorist

When to Seek Professional Help

Contact certified dog trainer or veterinary behaviorist for:

  • Aggression toward people or dogs
  • Severe anxiety or phobias
  • Resource guarding
  • Behaviors not improving with training
  • Any behavior you feel unsafe handling

Advanced Training Tips

Proofing Behaviors

Commands should work anywhere, not just at home. Gradually add the "3 Ds":

  • Duration: Sit for 5 seconds → 30 seconds → 2 minutes
  • Distance: Sit with you close → 5 feet away → across room
  • Distraction: Empty room → person walking by → dog park

Rule: Only increase ONE "D" at a time. If you add distance, reduce duration and distraction.

Fading Treats

Goal: Dog responds to cues without seeing treat first

  1. Once behavior is solid, use variable reinforcement
  2. Reward randomly (sometimes, not every time)
  3. Keep rewards high-quality when you do give them
  4. Never stop rewarding completely—maintain with occasional treats
  5. Always reward perfect responses in challenging situations

Adding Verbal and Hand Signals

  • Dogs read body language better than words
  • Teach hand signal first, add verbal later
  • Or pair both from beginning
  • Useful for senior dogs who go deaf

Maintaining Skills

  • Practice commands regularly even after "mastered"
  • Incorporate into daily life (sit before meals, down during TV)
  • Keep training fun with games and variety
  • Continue learning new tricks for mental stimulation

Training is Never "Done"

Training isn't a phase—it's a lifestyle. Keep reinforcing good behaviors throughout your dog's life. The bond and trust you build through training is one of the greatest gifts you can give your dog.

Fun Training Games

  • Hide and Seek: Practice recall in fun way
  • Find It: Hide treats, build nose work skills
  • Trick Training: Shake, roll over, spin—mental exercise
  • Obstacle Course: Tunnels, jumps, weave poles
  • Name that Toy: Teach names of different toys

Final Thoughts

Training is about building a relationship based on trust, communication, and mutual respect. Every minute you invest in positive training strengthens your bond and creates a happier, more confident dog who's a joy to live with.

Key Takeaways

  • ✅ Positive reinforcement builds trust and speeds learning
  • ✅ Consistency, timing, and patience are essential
  • ✅ Short, frequent training sessions beat long, exhausting ones
  • ✅ Socialization during puppyhood shapes adult temperament
  • ✅ Problem behaviors need prevention, not punishment
  • ✅ Training is a lifelong journey, not a destination

Remember: Every dog can learn. Some just need more time and creativity in training approaches. Celebrate small victories, stay patient, and enjoy the journey together!
