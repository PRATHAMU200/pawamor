Cat Health Warning Signs

Know When Your Cat Needs Veterinary Care

12 min read All Cat Owners Essential Guide

Cats are masters at hiding illness. In the wild, showing weakness makes them vulnerable to predators. This survival instinct means your cat may be seriously ill before showing obvious symptoms. Learning to recognize subtle warning signs can save your cat's life.

Call Emergency Vet Immediately If:

  • Difficulty breathing or rapid breathing
  • Collapse or unconsciousness
  • Severe bleeding that won't stop
  • Unable to urinate (especially male cats)
  • Seizures or convulsions
  • Suspected poisoning
  • Severe trauma (hit by car, high fall)
  • Straining to urinate with no urine production
  • Blue or pale gums
  • Body temperature below 100°F or above 104°F

Life-Threatening Emergency Signs

1. Urinary Blockage (Especially Male Cats)

This is one of the most common feline emergencies and can be fatal within 48-72 hours if untreated.

Critical Signs:
  • Straining in litter box with little to no urine
  • Crying or vocalizing while attempting to urinate
  • Blood in urine
  • Excessive licking of genital area
  • Vomiting (in advanced cases)
  • Lethargy and loss of appetite

Why It's Dangerous: Male cats' narrow urethras can become blocked by crystals, stones, or inflammation. This prevents urination, causing toxins to build up in the bloodstream and potentially leading to kidney failure and death.

Action: Rush to emergency vet immediately. This cannot wait until morning.

2. Difficulty Breathing

Any respiratory distress is a medical emergency in cats.

Signs:

  • Open-mouth breathing (cats should breathe through nose)
  • Rapid breathing (more than 40 breaths per minute at rest)
  • Labored breathing with visible chest/abdominal effort
  • Blue or pale gums/tongue
  • Extended neck and head low while breathing
  • Wheezing or raspy sounds

Possible Causes: Asthma, heart disease, fluid in lungs, airway obstruction, or pneumonia.

Action: Keep cat calm and get to emergency vet. Stress can worsen breathing problems.

3. Collapse or Unconsciousness

If your cat suddenly collapses, can't stand, or loses consciousness, this indicates a critical problem.

Possible Causes:

  • Heart disease (especially cardiomyopathy)
  • Blood clot (saddle thrombus)
  • Severe anemia
  • Poisoning
  • Seizure disorder
  • Low blood sugar (diabetes)

Action: Check if breathing and pulse are present. Transport to emergency vet immediately, keeping cat warm.

4. Seizures

While a single brief seizure may not be immediately life-threatening, seizures lasting more than 5 minutes or multiple seizures are emergencies.

What to Do:

  • Don't touch the cat during seizure (you may get bitten)
  • Clear area of objects cat could hit
  • Time the seizure
  • Keep environment quiet and dim
  • After seizure, keep cat calm and warm
  • Call vet immediately, especially if first seizure or if prolonged

Behavior Changes That Signal Illness

Cats are creatures of habit. Any significant change in behavior warrants attention.

Hiding More Than Usual

Sick cats often isolate themselves. If your normally social cat suddenly hides for extended periods, something is wrong.

Lethargy/Decreased Activity

Sleeping more than usual, not playing, reluctance to jump or climb stairs can indicate pain or illness.

Aggression or Irritability

A typically gentle cat becoming aggressive may be in pain. Cats can't tell us they hurt, so behavior changes.

Excessive Vocalization

Increased meowing, yowling, or crying (especially at night) can indicate pain, cognitive dysfunction, or hyperthyroidism.

Grooming Changes

Over-Grooming: Excessive licking leading to bald patches or skin irritation can indicate allergies, parasites, stress, or pain.

Under-Grooming: Unkempt, greasy, or matted coat suggests the cat feels too ill or painful to groom. Common in dental disease, arthritis, or obesity.

Eating and Drinking Changes

Not Eating (Anorexia)

Cats can develop serious liver problems (hepatic lipidosis) if they don't eat for 2-3 days, especially overweight cats.

Important: If your cat hasn't eaten for 24 hours, call your vet. If it's been 48 hours, this is an emergency.

Common Causes:

  • Dental disease (very common, often overlooked)
  • Nausea from various causes
  • Upper respiratory infection (can't smell food)
  • Kidney disease
  • Pancreatitis
  • Cancer
  • Stress or environmental changes

Eating More But Losing Weight

This combination is a red flag for several serious conditions:

  • Hyperthyroidism: Very common in senior cats. Other signs: hyperactivity, increased vocalization, drinking more water.
  • Diabetes: Increased appetite, thirst, and urination with weight loss.
  • Intestinal parasites: Especially in younger cats or those with outdoor access.
  • Malabsorption disorders: Cat can't absorb nutrients properly.

Drinking Excessively (Polydipsia)

If you're refilling the water bowl more frequently or your cat is constantly at the water bowl, this needs veterinary attention.

Common Causes:

  • Kidney disease (very common in senior cats)
  • Diabetes
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Liver disease

How to Monitor: Normal water intake is about 60ml per kg of body weight daily. A 4kg cat should drink about 240ml (1 cup) per day.

Litter Box Issues

Litter box changes are often the first sign of health problems. Never punish litter box issues without ruling out medical causes.

Urinating Outside Litter Box

Medical Causes to Rule Out:

  • Urinary tract infection (UTI)
  • Bladder stones or crystals
  • Kidney disease
  • Diabetes
  • Arthritis (painful to get in/out of box)
  • Cognitive dysfunction in senior cats

Warning Signs:

  • Frequent trips to litter box with small amounts of urine
  • Blood in urine
  • Straining or crying while urinating
  • Urinating in unusual places (bathtubs, sinks, cool surfaces)

Diarrhea or Constipation

Diarrhea is urgent if:

  • Contains blood
  • Cat is lethargic or vomiting
  • Lasts more than 24 hours
  • Kitten has diarrhea (they dehydrate quickly)

Constipation is urgent if:

  • No bowel movement for 48-72 hours
  • Straining repeatedly with no results
  • Vomiting along with constipation
  • Lethargy or loss of appetite
💡 Pro Tip: Take a photo of abnormal urine or stool before cleaning it up. Your vet will want to know color, consistency, and whether there's blood.

Physical Warning Signs

Weight Changes

Weight Loss: Loss of more than 10% body weight is significant. For a 4kg cat, that's only 400g, which can be hard to notice. Weigh monthly.

Weight Gain: Obesity is a serious health issue leading to diabetes, arthritis, and liver disease.

Vomiting

Occasional hairball vomiting (1-2 times monthly) is normal. Anything more frequent is not.

See Vet If:

  • Vomiting more than once weekly
  • Vomiting contains blood
  • Projectile vomiting
  • Vomiting accompanied by lethargy, diarrhea, or loss of appetite
  • Unable to keep water down

Eye Changes

Sudden Blindness

Dilated pupils, bumping into things, reluctance to move. Can indicate high blood pressure or retinal detachment. Emergency.

Third Eyelid Visible

The white/pink membrane in corner of eye showing across part of eye often indicates illness or pain.

Discharge or Squinting

Green/yellow discharge, holding eye closed, or pawing at eye suggests infection or injury.

Cloudy Eyes

Can indicate glaucoma, cataracts, or corneal problems. Needs prompt veterinary attention.

Mouth and Dental Issues

Dental disease affects 70% of cats by age 3. Signs include:

  • Bad breath
  • Drooling or dropping food
  • Pawing at mouth
  • Red or swollen gums
  • Reluctance to eat dry food
  • Eating on one side of mouth

Why It Matters: Dental disease is painful and bacteria can spread to heart, kidneys, and liver.

Breathing Problems

Normal vs. Abnormal Breathing

Normal: 20-30 breaths per minute at rest, through nose, quiet, and effortless.

How to Count: Watch chest rise and fall. One rise and fall = one breath. Count for 30 seconds and multiply by 2.

Abnormal Signs:

  • Open-mouth breathing when not hot or stressed
  • Panting (cats don't pant like dogs)
  • Noisy breathing (wheezing, crackling)
  • Using abdominal muscles to breathe
  • Rapid breathing at rest (over 40 breaths/minute)

Common Causes

  • Asthma: Wheezing, coughing, difficulty breathing. Can be life-threatening during attacks.
  • Heart Disease: Often no symptoms until advanced. May see rapid breathing, weakness, or blue-tinged gums.
  • Pleural Effusion: Fluid around lungs. Causes severe breathing difficulty.
  • Upper Respiratory Infection: Sneezing, nasal discharge, congestion.

Recognizing Pain in Cats

Cats hide pain very effectively. Look for subtle signs:

Body Language

Hunched posture, tucked tail, ears back, tense face, reluctance to move or jump.

Behavior Changes

Hiding, aggression when touched, decreased grooming, sleeping more.

Vocalization

Increased meowing/yowling, or becoming unusually quiet.

Appetite Loss

Not eating or being hesitant to eat (especially with dental pain).

Common Pain Sources

  • Arthritis: Very common in senior cats. Signs: difficulty jumping, stiff gait, reluctance to use stairs.
  • Dental Disease: Painful but often overlooked. Cat may still eat despite pain.
  • Urinary Issues: Painful urination, frequent trips to litter box.
  • Digestive Problems: Painful abdomen, hunched posture.
Never give cats human pain medication! Aspirin, ibuprofen, and acetaminophen are toxic to cats. Always consult your vet for safe pain management.

Senior Cat Warning Signs (7+ Years)

Older cats are prone to specific health issues. Watch for:

Kidney Disease (Very Common)

Signs:

  • Drinking and urinating more
  • Weight loss despite normal appetite
  • Vomiting
  • Bad breath (ammonia smell)
  • Lethargy

Hyperthyroidism

Signs:

  • Weight loss with increased appetite
  • Hyperactivity or restlessness
  • Increased drinking/urination
  • Vomiting or diarrhea
  • Unkempt coat
  • Rapid heart rate

Cognitive Dysfunction (Cat Dementia)

Signs:

  • Disorientation (getting lost in familiar places)
  • Changes in sleep-wake cycle
  • Yowling, especially at night
  • Litter box accidents
  • Changes in interaction with family

Cancer Warning Signs

  • Lumps or bumps that grow or change
  • Sores that don't heal
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Loss of appetite
  • Difficulty eating or swallowing
  • Persistent lameness or stiffness
  • Difficulty breathing, urinating, or defecating

Prevention and Early Detection

At-Home Health Monitoring

Weekly Checks:

  • Body condition: Run hands over body feeling for lumps, bumps, or weight changes
  • Coat condition: Should be clean and glossy
  • Eye check: Clear, bright, no discharge
  • Ear check: Clean, no odor or discharge
  • Gum check: Should be pink, not pale or red

Daily Observations:

  • Monitor litter box habits (frequency, amount, consistency)
  • Watch eating and drinking patterns
  • Notice energy levels and behavior
  • Check for vomiting or diarrhea

Monthly:

  • Weigh your cat (consistent method, same scale)
  • Note any behavioral changes

Regular Veterinary Care

Wellness Exams:

  • Kittens: Monthly until 4 months, then at 6 months and 1 year
  • Adult cats (1-7 years): Annual exam
  • Senior cats (7-10 years): Every 6 months
  • Geriatric cats (10+ years): Every 4-6 months

Why Regular Exams Matter: Many serious diseases (kidney disease, diabetes, thyroid issues) can be detected early through routine blood work before symptoms appear. Early detection dramatically improves outcomes.

Senior Cat Blood Work

For cats 7+, annual blood work screening is crucial. It can detect kidney disease, liver issues, diabetes, and thyroid problems before they cause symptoms. Early detection = better prognosis and quality of life.

When to Call the Vet

Emergency (Go Immediately)

  • Difficulty breathing
  • Unable to urinate (especially males)
  • Collapse or unconsciousness
  • Seizures
  • Severe trauma
  • Suspected poisoning
  • Profuse bleeding
  • Severe pain

Call Today (Within Hours)

  • Not eating for 24+ hours
  • Vomiting multiple times
  • Diarrhea for more than 24 hours
  • Blood in urine or stool
  • Straining to urinate or defecate
  • Severe lethargy
  • Eye injury or sudden vision changes
  • Difficulty walking or paralysis

Schedule Appointment (Within Days)

  • Gradual weight loss or gain
  • Changes in drinking/urinating
  • Behavior changes
  • Litter box issues
  • Bad breath or dental problems
  • Skin issues or excessive grooming
  • Limping or stiffness
  • Lumps or bumps

🚨 Trust Your Instincts

You know your cat better than anyone. If something feels wrong, don't hesitate to call your vet. It's always better to be cautious. Vets would rather you call unnecessarily than wait too long during a real emergency.

Final Thoughts

Cats depend on us to notice when something is wrong. By learning these warning signs and monitoring your cat's health, you become their best advocate. Early detection and prompt veterinary care can add years of quality life to your feline companion.

Remember: When in doubt, call your vet. There are no silly questions when it comes to your cat's health.
