Critical techniques that could save your pet's life - from CPR to choking and poisoning

CRITICAL NOTICE

Emergency first aid is NOT a substitute for veterinary care. These techniques are meant to stabilize your pet until you can reach professional help. Always call your vet or emergency clinic while providing first aid.

Emergency Basics

In a pet emergency, every second counts. Knowing basic first aid can make the difference between life and death while you're getting your pet to professional care.

The Golden Rule: Stay Calm

Your pet can sense your anxiety. Staying calm helps you think clearly and keeps your pet calmer too, which is crucial during emergencies.

Emergency Action Steps

  1. Assess the situation: Check if it's safe for you to approach
  2. Call for help: Have someone call the vet while you provide first aid
  3. Secure your pet: Even gentle pets may bite when in pain or scared
  4. Provide first aid: Address life-threatening issues first
  5. Transport safely: Keep pet warm and immobilized if needed

Emergency Numbers

Program these numbers into your phone NOW:

  • Your regular veterinarian
  • 24-hour emergency vet clinic
  • India Poison Control (AIIMS): 1800-116-117 or +91-11-2659-3268
  • Veterinary Poison Helpline (India): Contact your nearest veterinary hospital

Priority Assessment (ABC)

  • Airway: Is it clear? Can they breathe?
  • Breathing: Are they breathing? What's the rate and quality?
  • Circulation: Do they have a pulse? Check gums for color

Pet First Aid Kit Essentials

Basic Supplies

  • Gauze pads and rolls (sterile, various sizes)
  • Adhesive tape (medical, non-stick)
  • Cotton balls and swabs
  • Bandage scissors (blunt-tipped)
  • Tweezers (for removing debris, ticks)
  • Digital thermometer (rectal for pets)
  • Disposable gloves
  • Towels and blankets
  • Muzzle or soft cloth (even gentle pets may bite when injured)

Medications & Solutions

  • Saline solution (wound cleaning)
  • Hydrogen peroxide 3% (induce vomiting - ONLY if vet instructs)
  • Antibiotic ointment (check with vet for pet-safe options)
  • Styptic powder (stop nail bleeding)
  • Eye wash solution

Important Documents

  • Copy of vaccination records
  • List of current medications
  • Pet insurance information
  • Emergency contact numbers
  • Recent photo of your pet

Never Use on Pets

  • ❌ Human pain relievers (acetaminophen, ibuprofen - TOXIC)
  • ❌ Alcohol or peroxide on wounds (tissue damage)
  • ❌ Ointments with corticosteroids (unless prescribed)

CPR & Rescue Breathing

When to Perform CPR

Only if pet has NO heartbeat and is NOT breathing. Check for pulse on inner thigh (femoral artery) or chest.

Before Starting CPR

  1. Ensure scene is safe
  2. Check for responsiveness (tap, call name)
  3. Check airway - remove any visible obstruction
  4. Check breathing - watch chest movement
  5. Check pulse - inner thigh or chest
  6. Have someone call emergency vet

CPR for Dogs

Step 1: Position

  • Lay dog on RIGHT side on firm surface
  • Extend head and neck to open airway
  • For large dogs, position hands on widest part of chest
  • For small dogs (<10kg), one-handed chest compressions

Step 2: Compressions

  • Large dogs: Compress chest 1/3 to 1/2 depth
  • Small dogs: Wrap hand around chest, compress
  • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute
  • Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 rescue breaths

Step 3: Rescue Breaths

  • Close dog's mouth, seal your mouth over their nose
  • Blow steadily for 1 second (watch chest rise)
  • Give 2 breaths after every 30 compressions
  • If chest doesn't rise, reposition airway and try again

Continue CPR until:

  • Pet starts breathing on their own
  • You reach veterinary care
  • You're physically unable to continue
  • 20 minutes have passed without response

CPR for Cats

  • Position on right side, extend neck
  • Wrap hand around chest, compress 1/3 to 1/2 depth
  • Rate: 120-150 compressions per minute
  • 30 compressions : 2 rescue breaths
  • Seal mouth over cat's nose and mouth together

Get Certified

Consider taking a pet first aid/CPR class. Hands-on practice with a mannequin is invaluable. Many local animal organizations offer these courses.

Choking Emergency

Signs of Choking

  • Pawing at mouth
  • Excessive drooling
  • Gagging, retching sounds
  • Blue/gray gums (lack of oxygen)
  • Difficulty breathing or silent breathing attempts
  • Panic, distress

What to Do

Step 1: Check the Mouth

  • Be careful - choking pets may bite involuntarily
  • Open mouth wide, look inside with flashlight
  • If object visible and loose, try to remove with fingers or tweezers
  • Do NOT: Push object deeper or reach blindly

Step 2: Back Blows

  • Position pet with head lower than body
  • Use heel of hand between shoulder blades
  • Give 5 firm blows
  • Check mouth after each set

Step 3: Heimlich Maneuver

For Dogs:

  • Small dogs: Hold upright, back against your chest
  • Large dogs: Stand behind, arms around belly
  • Make fist just below ribcage
  • Quick upward thrusts (5 times)
  • Check mouth for dislodged object

For Cats:

  • Hold with back against your chest
  • One hand on each side of ribcage
  • Quick inward and upward squeezes

Rush to Vet Immediately

Even if object is dislodged, get veterinary care. Choking can cause internal damage and swelling that develops later.

Bleeding Control

Types of Bleeding

  • Arterial: Bright red, spurting - MOST URGENT
  • Venous: Dark red, steady flow - URGENT
  • Capillary: Oozing - Less urgent

External Bleeding

Direct Pressure Method

  1. Wear gloves if possible
  2. Place clean gauze or cloth directly on wound
  3. Apply firm, constant pressure for 3-5 minutes
  4. Don't peek - lifting pressure restarts bleeding
  5. If blood soaks through, add more gauze on top (don't remove)
  6. Once bleeding slows, bandage firmly (not too tight)

Severe Bleeding

  • Elevate wound above heart level if possible
  • Apply pressure to pressure points (between wound and heart)
  • Use pressure bandage: gauze + firmly wrapped bandage
  • Check circulation: Toes should be warm, pink

Nail Bleeding

  • Apply styptic powder or cornstarch
  • Press firmly for 30 seconds
  • Keep pet calm and still for 30 minutes

Never Use Tourniquets

Tourniquets can cause permanent damage and should only be used by trained professionals in life-threatening limb injuries.

Internal Bleeding Signs

  • Pale or white gums
  • Weakness, collapse
  • Distended abdomen
  • Rapid breathing and heart rate
  • Coughing blood or blood in urine/stool

Internal bleeding is CRITICAL - immediate vet care required!

Poisoning Emergency

Common Pet Poisons

  • Foods: Chocolate, xylitol, grapes/raisins, onions, garlic, alcohol
  • Medications: Human pain relievers, antidepressants, ADD/ADHD meds
  • Household: Antifreeze, rat poison, cleaning products
  • Plants: Lilies (cats), sago palm, oleander, azalea
  • Other: Insecticides, fertilizers, certain essential oils

Signs of Poisoning

  • Vomiting or diarrhea
  • Excessive drooling
  • Tremors or seizures
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Lethargy or hyperactivity
  • Loss of appetite
  • Unusual bleeding

Call Poison Control FIRST

Before doing anything, call Pet Poison Helpline (855-764-7661) or ASPCA (888-426-4435). They'll tell you exactly what to do based on the specific poison.

What to Do

  1. Identify the poison: Bring package/plant to phone/vet
  2. Call poison control: They'll guide you
  3. Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed (can cause more damage)
  4. If on skin/fur: Bathe immediately with mild soap
  5. If in eyes: Flush with saline or water for 15 minutes
  6. Get to vet immediately with poison info

Do NOT Induce Vomiting If:

  • Pet is unconscious or having seizures
  • Poison is caustic (bleach, drain cleaner, acids)
  • Poison is petroleum-based (gasoline, paint thinner)
  • More than 2 hours have passed

Information to Provide

  • Pet's species, breed, age, weight
  • Exact substance ingested (product name)
  • Amount consumed (estimate)
  • Time of exposure
  • Symptoms observed

Heatstroke Emergency

High-Risk Factors

  • Dogs in hot cars (even with windows cracked)
  • Exercise in hot/humid weather
  • Brachycephalic breeds (flat faces - Pugs, Bulldogs)
  • Obesity, thick coats
  • Senior pets, heart conditions

Signs of Heatstroke

  • Heavy panting, difficulty breathing
  • Excessive drooling, thick saliva
  • Bright red tongue and gums
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Vomiting, diarrhea (possibly bloody)
  • Weakness, collapse
  • Seizures, loss of consciousness

Critical Temperature

Normal: 100-102.5°F (37.8-39.2°C) | Emergency: Above 104°F (40°C) | Life-threatening: Above 106°F (41°C)

Immediate Actions

Step 1: Move to Cool Area

  • Get out of heat immediately
  • Move to air-conditioned room or shade
  • Turn on fans for air circulation

Step 2: Start Cooling (Gradually)

  • Apply cool (NOT ice cold) water to body
  • Focus on: Paws, belly, armpits, neck
  • Place cool, wet towels (change frequently)
  • Offer small amounts of cool water to drink (don't force)
  • DO NOT: Use ice or ice water (too rapid cooling is dangerous)

Step 3: Monitor & Transport

  • Take rectal temperature every 5 minutes
  • Stop cooling when temp reaches 103°F (39.4°C)
  • Get to vet immediately (even if pet seems recovered)
  • Keep air conditioning on during transport

Always See a Vet

Even if your pet appears to recover, heatstroke can cause delayed organ damage. Veterinary examination is critical.

Seizures

What a Seizure Looks Like

  • Falling over, loss of consciousness
  • Stiff, rigid body or violent jerking movements
  • Paddling legs
  • Drooling, foaming at mouth
  • Loss of bladder/bowel control
  • Staring, unresponsive

During a Seizure

  1. Stay calm - seizures usually stop on their own in 1-2 minutes
  2. Time the seizure - note start time
  3. Keep pet safe:
    • Move furniture, objects away
    • Don't restrain pet
    • DON'T put hands near mouth (they won't swallow tongue)
    • Dim lights, reduce noise
  4. Protect head: Place soft towel or pillow underneath
  5. Video if possible - helps vet diagnose

After a Seizure

  • Keep pet calm and quiet
  • Speak softly, provide comfort
  • Allow them to rest in a dark, quiet area
  • Don't offer food/water immediately (choking risk)
  • Monitor closely for 24 hours

Emergency Vet Immediately If:

  • Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
  • Multiple seizures in 24 hours
  • Pet doesn't fully recover between seizures
  • First-time seizure
  • Difficulty breathing after seizure

Fractures & Injuries

Signs of Fracture

  • Won't put weight on limb
  • Limb at odd angle
  • Swelling, bruising
  • Visible bone (open fracture)
  • Crying when touched

What to Do

  1. Minimize movement: Keep pet as still as possible
  2. Muzzle if necessary: Injured pets may bite
  3. Don't try to set bone or realign limb
  4. For open fractures:
    • Cover wound with sterile gauze
    • Don't clean or apply ointment
    • Bandage gently to protect
  5. Stabilize if transporting long distance:
    • Use rolled towel or newspaper as splint
    • Secure above and below fracture site
    • Not too tight - check circulation
  6. Transport carefully: Use blanket as stretcher for large dogs

Spinal Injury Suspicion

Signs: Paralysis, can't stand, loss of pain sensation

Critical: EXTREME care to prevent further damage

  • Keep spine absolutely straight
  • Slide stiff board under pet
  • Tape pet to board to prevent movement
  • Two people to carry if possible

When to Go to Emergency Vet

Life-Threatening - GO IMMEDIATELY

  • ❗ Difficulty breathing or not breathing
  • ❗ Unconscious or unresponsive
  • ❗ Seizures (especially lasting >5 min)
  • ❗ Severe bleeding that won't stop
  • ❗ Bloated, distended abdomen (especially large dogs)
  • ❗ Unable to urinate or defecate
  • ❗ Suspected poisoning
  • ❗ Hit by car or major trauma
  • ❗ Heatstroke
  • ❗ Snake bite
  • ❗ Eye injury or sudden blindness

Urgent - Within Hours

  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhea (especially with blood)
  • Not eating/drinking for 24+ hours
  • Lethargy, weakness
  • Painful abdomen
  • Straining to urinate/defecate
  • Limping, inability to walk
  • Continuous crying or signs of pain

Monitor Closely - Call Vet for Advice

  • Mild vomiting/diarrhea (1-2 episodes)
  • Decreased appetite
  • Mild lethargy
  • Minor wounds
  • Itching, skin irritation

When in Doubt, Call

If you're unsure whether something is an emergency, call your vet or emergency clinic. They can help you assess whether immediate care is needed. It's always better to be safe!

Prevention is Best

Emergency Preparedness

  • Keep first aid kit stocked and accessible
  • Know location of 24-hour emergency vet
  • Pet-proof your home
  • Keep toxic substances locked away
  • Microchip and ID tags updated
  • Take pet first aid course

Regular Health Monitoring

  • Know your pet's normal vital signs
  • Annual vet check-ups (senior pets: twice yearly)
  • Stay current on vaccinations
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Regular dental care

Final Thoughts

Hope for the best, prepare for the worst. Knowledge of these emergency techniques can save your pet's life, but the goal is never having to use them. Stay vigilant, pet-proof your environment, and maintain regular vet care. Your preparedness is an act of love.
