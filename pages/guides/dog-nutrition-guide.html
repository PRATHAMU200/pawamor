Understanding Dog Nutrition

Proper nutrition is the foundation of your dog's health, energy levels, and longevity. Just like humans, dogs require a balanced diet containing proteins, fats, carbohydrates, vitamins, and minerals in the right proportions.

Essential Nutrients for Dogs

  • Proteins: Building blocks for muscles, organs, and immune system. Should comprise 18-25% of adult dog diet
  • Fats: Energy source and essential for absorption of vitamins. Required: 10-15% of diet
  • Carbohydrates: Provide energy and fiber for digestive health
  • Vitamins & Minerals: Support immune function, bone health, and metabolic processes
  • Water: Most critical nutrient - dogs need constant access to fresh water

Pro Tip

Dogs are omnivores, not carnivores! They can digest and benefit from both animal and plant-based nutrients, making a varied diet ideal.

Types of Dog Food

1. Dry Food (Kibble)

Pros: Cost-effective, convenient, helps clean teeth, long shelf life
Cons: May contain more fillers, less appealing to picky eaters
Best For: Most dogs, budget-conscious owners, dental health

2. Wet Food (Canned)

Pros: High moisture content, more palatable, easier to digest
Cons: More expensive, shorter shelf life once opened, can cause dental issues
Best For: Senior dogs, picky eaters, dogs with dental problems

3. Fresh/Raw Food

Pros: Minimal processing, high-quality ingredients, good for allergies
Cons: Most expensive, requires refrigeration, food safety concerns
Best For: Dogs with allergies, owners committed to preparation

4. Homemade Diet

Pros: Complete control over ingredients, can accommodate special needs
Cons: Time-consuming, requires nutritional knowledge, balance challenges
Best For: Dogs with specific health conditions (with vet guidance)

Important

If considering a raw or homemade diet, ALWAYS consult with a veterinary nutritionist first to ensure balanced nutrition. Improper homemade diets can lead to serious deficiencies.

Feeding by Life Stage

Puppy Stage (0-12 months)

Puppies need food specifically formulated for growth with:

  • Higher protein (22-32%) for muscle development
  • Higher fat (10-25%) for energy and brain development
  • Proper calcium-to-phosphorus ratio for bone growth
  • DHA for cognitive development

Feeding Schedule:

  • 8-12 weeks: 4 meals per day
  • 3-6 months: 3 meals per day
  • 6-12 months: 2 meals per day

Adult Stage (1-7 years)

Adult dogs require maintenance nutrition:

  • Protein: 18-25% for muscle maintenance
  • Fat: 10-15% for energy
  • Moderate calories based on activity level

Feeding Schedule: 2 meals per day (morning and evening)

Senior Stage (7+ years)

Senior dogs need adjusted nutrition:

  • Lower calories to prevent weight gain (reduced metabolism)
  • Higher quality protein to maintain muscle mass
  • Joint support: Glucosamine and omega-3 fatty acids
  • Antioxidants for immune support
  • More fiber for digestive health

Feeding Schedule: 2-3 smaller meals per day (easier digestion)

Life Stage Protein % Fat % Meals/Day Calories/Day (20kg dog)
Puppy 22-32% 10-25% 3-4 1,200-1,500
Adult 18-25% 10-15% 2 900-1,100
Senior 20-28% 8-12% 2-3 700-900

Portion Control & Weight Management

Calculating Portions

Base feeding amounts on your dog's:

  • Current Weight: Not ideal weight - adjust as they reach target
  • Activity Level: Active dogs need 20-30% more calories
  • Metabolism: Some breeds have faster metabolisms
  • Body Condition: Can you feel ribs easily? Visible waist from above?

General Guidelines

  • Small Dogs (0-10kg): 150-400 calories/day
  • Medium Dogs (10-25kg): 400-1,000 calories/day
  • Large Dogs (25-45kg): 1,000-1,500 calories/day
  • Giant Dogs (45kg+): 1,500-2,500 calories/day

Feeding Tip

Use a measuring cup! Free-feeding or eyeballing portions is the #1 cause of pet obesity. Follow packaging guidelines as a starting point, then adjust based on your dog's body condition.

Signs Your Dog is Overweight

  • Cannot feel ribs easily without pressing
  • No visible waist when viewed from above
  • Abdominal sagging or fat deposits
  • Difficulty breathing or exercise intolerance

Signs Your Dog is Underweight

  • Ribs, spine, and hip bones easily visible
  • Prominent waist and abdominal tuck
  • Lack of muscle mass
  • Low energy levels

Reading Food Labels

What to Look For

Quality Protein Sources (First 3 Ingredients):

  • ✅ Named meats: Chicken, beef, salmon, turkey, lamb
  • ✅ Meat meals: Chicken meal, fish meal (concentrated protein)
  • ❌ Avoid: Generic "meat meal" or "animal by-products"

Healthy Whole Grains/Carbs:

  • ✅ Brown rice, oats, sweet potato, peas, lentils
  • ❌ Avoid: Corn, wheat, soy as primary ingredients (common allergens)

Beneficial Additions:

  • Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil, flaxseed)
  • Probiotics for digestive health
  • Glucosamine for joint health
  • Antioxidants (vitamins E and C)
  • Named vegetables and fruits

Red Flags to Avoid

  • ❌ BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin (chemical preservatives)
  • ❌ Artificial colors and flavors
  • ❌ Excessive fillers (corn gluten meal, wheat mill run)
  • ❌ Generic terms: "meat," "animal fat," "poultry by-product"
  • ❌ High sodium content

Certification Matters

Look for foods that meet AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials) standards. The label should state: "Complete and balanced nutrition" or "Formulated to meet AAFCO standards."

Special Dietary Needs

Common Food Allergies

Most common allergens in dogs:

  • Beef, dairy, chicken, wheat, corn, soy, eggs

Symptoms: Itchy skin, ear infections, digestive upset, hot spots
Solution: Limited ingredient diet or novel protein (duck, venison, fish)

Weight Management Diets

  • Lower calories (10-20% reduction)
  • Higher fiber for satiety
  • Maintained protein to preserve muscle
  • L-carnitine to support metabolism

Sensitive Stomach Diets

  • Highly digestible proteins (chicken, fish)
  • Limited ingredients
  • Added probiotics and prebiotics
  • Moderate fat content

Grain-Free Diets

Important Update

Recent FDA warnings link some grain-free diets (especially those high in peas, lentils, potatoes) to heart disease (DCM). Unless your dog has a diagnosed grain allergy, grain-inclusive diets are generally recommended. Consult your veterinarian.

Treats & Supplements

Healthy Treat Guidelines

  • Treats should be less than 10% of daily calorie intake
  • Use for training reinforcement and special occasions
  • Choose low-calorie options: carrots, green beans, apple slices (no seeds)
  • Avoid human food high in fat, salt, or sugar

Safe Human Foods for Dogs

  • ✅ Carrots, sweet potato, pumpkin (cooked)
  • ✅ Blueberries, apple slices (no core/seeds), watermelon
  • ✅ Plain cooked chicken, turkey, salmon
  • ✅ Plain yogurt, peanut butter (xylitol-free)
  • ✅ Cooked eggs, green beans

Toxic Foods - NEVER Feed

Dangerous/Toxic

  • ❌ Chocolate, caffeine, alcohol
  • ❌ Grapes, raisins, currants
  • ❌ Onions, garlic, chives, leeks
  • ❌ Xylitol (artificial sweetener - HIGHLY toxic)
  • ❌ Macadamia nuts, walnuts
  • ❌ Avocado, cherry pits, apple seeds
  • ❌ Raw yeast dough, moldy food

Supplements

Quality commercial foods are usually complete. Supplements may benefit:

  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Skin/coat health, anti-inflammatory
  • Glucosamine & Chondroitin: Joint support for seniors or large breeds
  • Probiotics: Digestive health, immune support
  • Multivitamins: Only if recommended by vet for specific deficiency

Supplement Safety

Always consult your veterinarian before adding supplements. More isn't better - some vitamins (A, D) can be toxic in excess.

Hydration & Water Needs

Daily Water Requirements

Dogs need approximately 1 ounce of water per pound of body weight daily (or 60-70ml per kg).

  • 10 kg dog: ~600-700ml per day
  • 20 kg dog: ~1.2-1.4 liters per day
  • 30 kg dog: ~1.8-2.1 liters per day

Factors Increasing Water Needs

  • Hot weather and exercise
  • Dry food diet (wet food provides moisture)
  • Lactation (nursing mothers)
  • Illness (fever, diarrhea, vomiting)

Signs of Dehydration

  • Dry gums and nose
  • Loss of skin elasticity (skin doesn't snap back when pinched)
  • Sunken eyes
  • Lethargy and weakness
  • Dark-colored urine

Water Tips

  • Always provide fresh, clean water
  • Change water daily and clean bowls regularly
  • Multiple water stations in large homes
  • Consider a water fountain - many dogs prefer running water
  • Carry collapsible bowl and water on walks

Final Nutrition Tips

Transitioning Foods

When changing dog food, transition gradually over 7-10 days to avoid digestive upset:

  • Days 1-2: 75% old food, 25% new food
  • Days 3-4: 50% old food, 50% new food
  • Days 5-6: 25% old food, 75% new food
  • Day 7+: 100% new food

Feeding Routine Best Practices

  • Feed at consistent times daily
  • Remove uneaten food after 15-20 minutes
  • Separate feeding areas for multiple dogs
  • Wait 30-60 minutes after meals before vigorous exercise (bloat risk)
  • Monitor weight monthly and adjust portions as needed

When to Consult Your Veterinarian

  • Sudden appetite changes or refusal to eat
  • Rapid weight loss or gain
  • Persistent digestive issues (vomiting, diarrhea)
  • Suspected food allergies or sensitivities
  • Planning dietary changes for health conditions
  • Questions about appropriate food for your dog's specific needs

Remember

Proper nutrition is one of the most important investments you can make in your dog's health and longevity. Every dog is unique - work with your veterinarian to find the perfect diet for your furry friend's individual needs.
